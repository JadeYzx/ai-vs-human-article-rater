The rapid expansion of social media platforms has fundamentally reshaped how audiences consume news, creating both new opportunities for access and persistent challenges related to trust and credibility.

In the United States and globally, social media has become a central gateway to news. Recent studies show that more than half of U.S. adults—approximately 53%—report getting news from social media at least occasionally, a figure that has remained relatively stable in recent years.  ￼ At the same time, social platforms have overtaken traditional outlets in prominence. Around 54% of Americans now access news via social media and video networks, surpassing television and dedicated news websites for the first time.  ￼

This shift is especially pronounced among younger audiences. Data indicates that reliance on social media as a primary news source is highest among people under 35, with platforms such as TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube playing a dominant role.  ￼ In fact, one in five U.S. adults now regularly obtains news from TikTok alone, reflecting the platform’s rapid growth as a news channel.  ￼ For many young users, news consumption is increasingly incidental rather than intentional, with 70% reporting that they encounter political news while browsing rather than actively seeking it out.  ￼

Video-based and algorithm-driven content has further accelerated these trends. Globally, consumption of social video for news has risen sharply, with usage increasing from just over half of audiences in 2020 to roughly two-thirds by 2025.  ￼ This transformation reflects broader changes in digital behavior, as audiences favor short-form, personalized, and easily shareable content formats.

However, the rise of social media as a news source has coincided with declining trust in information ecosystems. Surveys indicate that only 56% of U.S. adults express at least some trust in national news organizations, marking a significant decline over the past decade.  ￼ Trust in social media is even more fragile, particularly among older demographics, though younger users show nearly equal levels of trust in social platforms and traditional news outlets.  ￼

Compounding the issue is widespread concern about misinformation. More than half of respondents globally report difficulty distinguishing true from false information online, highlighting the growing complexity of digital news environments.  ￼ Social media platforms, while increasing access and diversity of viewpoints, also enable the rapid spread of unverified or misleading content, contributing to what researchers describe as fragmented and polarized information ecosystems.

Despite these concerns, audiences continue to rely heavily on social platforms for news, illustrating a paradox at the heart of modern media consumption. Convenience, speed, and personalization drive engagement, even as skepticism persists. Meanwhile, traditional news organizations are adapting by expanding their presence on social platforms, experimenting with new formats, and seeking to rebuild trust through transparency and audience engagement.

As digital consumption patterns continue to evolve, the relationship between social media and news remains complex. The growing dominance of platforms in shaping public understanding underscores the need for improved media literacy, stronger content moderation, and sustained efforts to enhance the credibility of information in an increasingly decentralized media landscape.
